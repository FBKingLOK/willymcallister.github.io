Getting started

Drag components from the parts bin onto the main screen.

Add wires by dragging between terminals.

Tap on the Rotate icon or type "r" to rotate a component.

Tap on $\times$ or type BACKSPACE to delete a component.

Double-tap on a component to change its properties.

Add a ground symbol (triangle shape at the top of the parts bin).

Add voltage or current probes to the nodes you want to plot. Select DC, AC, or TRAN from the menu to simulate the circuit.

Touch (tablet or smartphone):

  • One finger: selects components or drags a selection rectangle
  • Two fingers: pans schematic window

Build the circuit shown above.

To create the RC circuit shown above, follow these steps starting from a blank schematic.

  • Add components (voltage source, resistor, capacitor) from the parts bin on the right onto the schematic. Click on a component in the bin, then click on the schematic. Or, click down on a component in the bin and drag it to the schematic.
  • Add components (voltage source, resistor, capacitor) from the parts bin on the right onto the schematic. Click on a component in the bin, then click on the schematic. Or, click down on a component in the bin and drag it to the schematic.
  • Select the resistor by clicking on it. It will turn green. While it is selected, hit the "r" key on the keyboard to make it rotate to horizontal. You can also click on the $\circlearrowright$ rotation arrow icon. Do repeated rotations to see what happens.
  • Position the components on the page by dragging them around.
  • Add wires by dragging between the connection points on the components. If you make a mistake, hit the backspace/delete key on your keyboard, or click on the $\large \times$ icon.
  • Specify which node is ground by dragging the ground symbol from the parts bin (triangle with a stick) and connect it to the bottom of the voltage source.
  • Adjust the component properties. Double-click on the voltage source and change its type to "step". Give it a name if you want. In the same way, change the resistor value to 1k, and the capacitor value to 1u. The k and u suffixes are short for $10^3$ and $10^{-6}$.
  • Add a voltage probe (the thing in the parts bin that looks like a voltage probe) to the top node of the capacitor. This is the voltage we will simulate and plot.
  • Select an analysis to perform. For this example, let's do a transient analysis, so click on TRAN in the menu. Set the stop time to 10m. The suffix m stands for $10^{-3}$.
  • Click OK and the simulation is performed.

Help

Add a component: Tap on a part in the parts bin, then tap on the schematic to add.

Add a wire: Wires start at connection points (open circles). Touch on a connection to start a wire, drag, and release.

Select: Drag a rectangle to select components. Shift-click to include another component (desktop only).

Move: Touch to select, drag to new location.

Delete: Tap to select, then tap the $\large \times$ icon or hit BACKSPACE.

Rotate/Reflect: Tap to select, then tap on the Rotate icon or type the letter "r" to rotate 90. Repeat for more rotations and reflections.

Properties: Double-tap on a component to change its properties, like resistance or voltage.

Numeric suffixes: You can enter numbers in engineering notation:

T    10^12      m   10^-3 
G    10^9       u   10^-6  
M    10^6       n   10^-9  
k    10^3       p   10^-12
                f   10^-15

example: $1000$ can be entered by typing $1\text k$

Models

The simulator has simple models for semiconductor devices and an operational amplifier, with just a few adjustable parameters. For more sophisticated simulations, check out the other circuit simulator resources mentioned below.

System information

The circuit sandbox works best in a desktop or laptop environment. (It works on a tablet, but the download/import functions are not available.)

Desktop/laptop

The SAVE function saves a circuit text file (in JSON format) to the Download folder on your desktop computer. The OPEN function retrieves a circuit text file from anywhere you choose in your computer's file system. Your circuit is never transferred to Khan Academy servers. If you want to keep your work, be sure to SAVE your circuit before leaving the web page.

The downloaded netlist file is named ckt.txt. (text file, JSON, JavaScript Object Notation)

Tablets and other mobile devices

The simulator has a touch interface. The simulator saves one circuit file. When you SAVE a circuit, it is saved to a memory area inside your browser. When you OPEN the saved circuit, the circuit file is retrieved from browser memory. If you want to keep your work, be sure to SAVE your circuit before leaving the web page.

Other circuit simulators

The granddaddy of all circuit simulation programs is SPICE (Simulation Program with Integrated Circuit Emphasis). This program was created at the University of California, Berkeley in the 1970's. It was originally written in FORTRAN and has always been in the public domain.

The input to SPICE is a plain-text circuit description called the SPICE netlist. SPICE supports sophisticated models for non-linear semiconductor devices (diodes and many types of transistors). Over the years, many people have written front-end interfaces to SPICE that allow designers to create schematics by drawing them. The SPICE netlislt is automatically generated and submitted to SPICE for simulation. The results are retrieved from SPICE and plotted. Many of these packages can be found by searching the web for "circuit simulator."

An excellent version of SPICE is available from Linear Technology, a semiconductor manufacturing company in Milpitas, California. The program is called LTspice. LTspice runs on Windows, Macintosh, and Linux machines with Windows emulation. It is free to download and use."
